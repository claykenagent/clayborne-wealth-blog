# Best Budget Stock Trading Brokers 2024: Interactive Brokers vs Fidelity vs Webull (Under $100 Account Minimum)

You've decided to start investing. That's awesome.

But here's the thing nobody tells you: **the broker you choose could cost you thousands in hidden fees before you even make your first real trade.**

I've watched friends open accounts with popular brokers, only to discover months later that they'd been hemorrhaging money on spreads, data fees, and surprise charges they didn't know existed. One buddy lost nearly $2,000 annually to inactivity fees and per-trade commissions—money that could've been compounding in his portfolio instead.

If you're starting with less than $100, you need a broker that won't nickel-and-dime you to death. The question isn't just "which broker is cheapest?"—it's "which one respects my money and my time?"

Let me show you exactly what I found when I dug into the fee structures of the three biggest players in the budget trading space.

## The Problem: Hidden Trading Fees Are Costing You Thousands

Let's do some quick math.

If you trade just **2-3 times per week** at a broker charging $5 per trade:
- Weekly cost: $10–15
- Monthly cost: $40–60
- **Annual cost: $480–720**

Now multiply that by a decade, factor in the lost compound growth on those fees, and you're looking at **$5,000–$8,000 in pure waste**.

But that's just the obvious stuff.

Most brokers also charge:
- **Data feed fees** ($15–100/month for real-time quotes)
- **Inactivity fees** ($50–100/year if you don't trade enough)
- **Bid-ask spreads** (the invisible cost that kills day traders)
- **Wire transfer fees** ($25–50 per withdrawal)
- **Currency conversion fees** (if you trade international stocks)

The brokers don't advertise these loudly. They're buried in the fine print. And most new traders don't find out until it's too late.

**The stakes are real.** If you're starting your investing journey with $500–$2,000, you can't afford to waste 10–20% of it on fees before you even get started.

## The Agitation: What Happens If You Pick the Wrong Broker

Here's the scary part: **the wrong choice compounds over time.**

Let's say you open an account with Broker A (slightly expensive) instead of Broker B (cheap):

**Year 1:** You lose $500 to fees. Not amazing, but survivable.

**Year 5:** Those fees add up. But worse—the money you *could've invested* instead of paying fees would've grown at 10% annually. You're now behind by $3,000+ when you account for lost growth.

**Year 10:** You're looking at $8,000–$12,000 in opportunity cost.

That's not just money. That's a car. That's a down payment on a house. That's your kids' first year of college.

And here's what really stings: **you don't even notice it happening.** Fees are abstract. You don't get a bill that says "YOU LOST $500 TODAY." It's just... gone.

Most new traders don't realize until they're 3–6 months in, and by then they've already paid hundreds in unnecessary costs.

**The good news?** This is totally avoidable. You just need to know which brokers actually respect small accounts.

### 2. **Interactive Brokers: Best for Active Traders** 📊

**Why consider it:** If you're planning to trade 2+ times per week, Interactive Brokers' commission structure might save you money.

**The numbers:**
- Minimum account: $500
- Stock trades: $1 per 100 shares (capped at 0.5% of trade value)
- Options: $0.65 per contract
- Data feeds: FREE for basic, $10–50/month for premium
- Inactivity fee: $10/month if no activity in 3 months
- Tiered fee: Monthly fee of $20 is waived if commissions exceed $20

**What makes it special:**
Interactive Brokers is built for serious traders. Their platform is more complex, but the commission structure rewards frequent traders. If you're trading options or actively managing a portfolio, this could save you money versus Fidelity.

**Best for:** Active traders, options traders, people who want advanced charting tools and fast execution.

**Potential downside:** The $500 minimum excludes true beginners. And the interface has a learning curve.

### 3. **Webull: Best for Fractional Shares & Low Minimums** 🚀

**Why consider it:** Webull lets you start with almost nothing and offers extended trading hours.

**The numbers:**
- Minimum account: $1 (fractional shares available)
- Stock trades: FREE
- Fractional shares: FREE
- Extended trading hours: 4 AM–8 PM ET
- Data feeds: FREE (real-time)
- Inactivity fees: NONE

**What makes it special:**
Webull's big advantage is *fractional shares*. You can literally invest $5 and own a piece of Tesla or Amazon. Their app is also the most mobile-friendly for watching prices on the go.

They're also expanding features constantly, and their paper trading (practice) account is excellent for learning.

**Best for:** Absolute beginners with tiny amounts to invest, people who want to practice before committing real money, mobile-first traders.

**Potential downside:** Webull is newer and smaller than Fidelity. Some investors worry about long-term stability (though they've been around since 2017). Also, their data quality isn't quite as good as Interactive Brokers' for advanced traders.

## Which One Should You Actually Pick?

**Choose Fidelity if:** You want the safest choice, you're not sure how much you'll trade, or you value customer service.

**Choose Interactive Brokers if:** You plan to trade actively (2+ times/week) or want to trade options, and you have at least $500.

**Choose Webull if:** You're starting with under $100, you want to practice with fractional shares, or you want to trade outside regular market hours.

**My recommendation?** If this is your first time investing and you have less than $500, start with **Fidelity**. The zero minimum, zero fees, and excellent tools mean you can't make an expensive mistake. As you grow and trade more actively, you can always move to Interactive Brokers later.

## Mistake #1: Over-Leveraging Your Account (The Silent Account Killer)

You see a micro-cap stock up 40% in the last week. Your heart races. You think, "If I use a little leverage, I can turn $5,000 into $15,000 in one trade."

Then the stock drops 15%.

With leverage, that's a 45% loss on your account. Your $5,000 is now $2,750.

**Why this happens:** Leverage is the most seductive trap in trading. It feels like you're being smart and aggressive. It feels like you're maximizing opportunity. In reality, you're playing with dynamite.

**How to fix it:** 
Start with a simple rule: **Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account on a single trade.** If your account is $10,000, that means your maximum loss per trade should be $100-200. This sounds conservative—because it is. But conservative traders are the ones who still have accounts.

The best way to enforce this rule is to track it visually. You need a **trading journal**—something that shows you exactly how much you're risking on each trade before you enter it.

## Mistake #2: FOMO Entries (Fear of Missing Out Kills More Accounts Than Market Crashes)

A stock starts running. You see it +25% in 2 hours on some trading forum. Everyone's talking about it. You *have* to get in.

You buy at the top.

Within 48 hours, it's down 40%. You're down $1,500.

**Why this happens:** FOMO is a primal emotion. Our brains are wired to not want to miss out on the tribe. When everyone's talking about a stock, we feel like we're missing something. The technical analysis? The risk assessment? That goes out the window.

**How to fix it:**
Create a **wait list, not a watch list.** If a stock interests you:
1. Write down exactly what price you'll buy it at (based on your analysis, not emotion)
2. Set a price alert
3. Wait for it to come to your price

Most "hot" micro-caps that are already up 25% will pull back. When it does, you'll have a calm, logical entry instead of a panicked one.

The traders who consistently profit are the boring ones. They wait. They don't chase.

## Mistake #3: No Exit Plan (Hope is Not a Strategy)

You buy a micro-cap at $2.50. You tell yourself, "I'm going to sell at $3.50."

It runs to $3.40. You think, "It could hit $4.00."

It drops to $2.80. You think, "I'll wait for it to recover."

It's now $1.10. You're holding a massive loss because you never committed to an exit.

**Why this happens:** Traders fall in love with their positions. You've spent time researching the company. You've told friends you're investing in it. Admitting it was a bad trade feels like admitting *you* made a bad decision.

**How to fix it:**
Every trade needs a **target profit and a stop loss before you enter.** Not after. Not "when I feel like it." Before.

If you buy at $2.50:
- **Target:** $3.50 (40% gain)
- **Stop loss:** $2.25 (10% loss)

When you hit either one, you exit. No exceptions. No "but I think it will..."

This is where that trading journal becomes invaluable. Every single trade gets logged with your entry, target, and stop loss *before* you buy. It keeps you accountable.

## Mistake #4: Chasing Penny Stock Hype (The Most Profitable Trap Isn't Yours)

Someone on Reddit says a penny stock is "about to explode." They post screenshots of other people's profits. It feels inevitable that this stock will run.

You buy 10,000 shares at $0.45.

The stock runs to $0.47. You're up $200. You think you've found the goldmine.

Then volume dries up. It never runs again. You're stuck holding bags of shares that no one wants to buy.

**Why this happens:** Penny stocks are the most manipulated, least regulated trading instruments in retail finance. The people telling you about them often have a vested interest—they want *you* to buy so they can sell to you at a profit.

**How to fix it:**
If you're going to trade micro-caps, set strict rules:
1. **Minimum daily volume:** The stock must trade at least $100,000-500,000 per day
2. **Check the company financials:** Can you actually find revenue? Earnings? A real business?
3. **Avoid sub-$1 stocks** unless you have serious experience

A good book that breaks down exactly how these scams work is [*The Intelligent Investor* by Benjamin Graham](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0060555661?tag=thenestlovely-20)—it teaches you to think like an investor instead of a gambler.

For a deeper dive into risk management specifically, [*A Random Walk Down Wall Street*](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0393352242?tag=thenestlovely-20) shows why most people underestimate volatility in micro-caps.

## Mistake #5: Ignoring Liquidity (The Exit You Can't Take)

You're up 35% on a micro-cap stock. You want to sell. 

But there are no buyers at any reasonable price. The bid-ask spread is 15%. By the time you sell, you've lost half your gains to slippage.

Or worse: you're down 25% and you want to cut losses, but the stock barely trades. You're trapped.

**Why this happens:** Traders fall in love with the *potential* of a stock and ignore the practical reality of exiting it. Even if the stock price goes where you want, if there's no volume, you can't actually profit.

**How to fix it:**
Before you buy any micro-cap:
- Check the **average daily volume**
- Calculate what it means to sell your full position: If you own 5,000 shares and average daily volume is 50,000, you own 10% of a day's volume. You *will* move the price when you exit.
- Ask yourself: "Can I actually exit this position when I want to?"

If the answer is "not easily," don't buy it.

## The Real Cost of Waiting to Invest

Before we dive into specific funds, let's talk about what happens when you *don't* invest.

Imagine you have $500 sitting in a regular savings account. After one year, at a 0.01% interest rate, you've earned about 5 cents. After 10 years? You're up about 50 cents.

Now imagine that same $500 invested in a simple S&P 500 index fund. Historically, this fund returns about 10% per year on average (some years higher, some lower). Over 10 years, that $500 becomes roughly $1,297. Over 30 years? It grows to nearly $8,750.

That's the difference between doing nothing and taking action.

The math is compelling. But here's what really matters: **every month you delay is a month of compound growth you can never get back.** There's no way to recapture lost time in the market—only lost money.

## Why Index Funds Are Perfect for Beginners (Especially With Limited Cash)

You might be wondering: why index funds specifically?

Three reasons:

**1. They're diversified.** One fund can hold 500+ individual stocks. If one company tanks, it barely dents your portfolio.

**2. They have rock-bottom fees.** Most quality index funds charge 0.03–0.10% annually. That means you pay just $3–10 per year for every $10,000 invested. Compare that to actively managed funds (1–2% fees) and you're saving a fortune.

**3. They're proven.** Warren Buffett recommends them. Academic research shows they outperform 90% of professional fund managers over 15+ years.

For beginners with limited funds, index funds are the fastest path to building real wealth.

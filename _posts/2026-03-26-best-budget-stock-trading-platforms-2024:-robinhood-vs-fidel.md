**Total actual cost: ~$42.50 on a $300 investment. That's 14% gone.**

## The Head-to-Head Breakdown

### Robinhood: Perfect for Simplicity (With Caveats)

**Pros:**
- Dead-simple UI. Literally designed for someone who's never invested.
- Zero friction. Download app, fund account, buy stock in 90 seconds.
- Gamification can actually motivate beginners to learn.

**Cons:**
- Spreads are *not* the tightest. On a $300 position, you could lose $1-2 to spread alone.
- The simplicity is a double-edged sword—you have fewer research tools, so you might make worse decisions.
- Limited account types (no dedicated retirement accounts until recently).

**When to choose Robinhood:** You've already done your research, you know what you want to buy, and you want zero friction.

---

### Webull: Best for Active Traders (On a Budget)

**Pros:**
- Extended trading hours (4 AM – 8 PM ET). Catch early movers and after-hours news.
- Advanced charting tools for free (usually $70/month elsewhere).
- Paper trading account lets you practice without real money.

**Cons:**
- Spreads can be slightly wider than Fidelity or Charles Schwab.
- Interface is more complex—not ideal if you're brand new.
- Some users report slower customer service.

**When to choose Webull:** You're planning to trade more actively (weekly or daily) and want tools that scale as you learn.

---

### Fidelity: The Goldilocks Option

**Pros:**
- Rock-solid fundamentals across the board.
- Screeners and analysis tools rival platforms costing $100+/month.
- Fidelity's research reports and analyst picks are genuinely useful.
- If you eventually want to open a Roth IRA or 401(k) account, Fidelity integrates seamlessly.

**Cons:**
- Design feels corporate. Some beginners find it less fun than Robinhood.
- Slightly steeper learning curve for the tools.

**When to choose Fidelity:** You're serious about learning, you plan to invest long-term, and you want one platform you'll never outgrow.
